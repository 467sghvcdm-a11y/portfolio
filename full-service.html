The problem

TurboTax Live launched with a promise and a workflow that couldn't keep it. By Tax Year 2021, the average customer waited 18 days from their first contact with a tax prep assistant to a filed return. For dissatisfied customers it was 21 days. 30% named wait time as the reason. 16% of all support calls were customers asking where their expert was.

Only 32% of Full Service customers returned the following year.

The product had been designed around the expert's workflow, and it showed. Customers uploaded documents, got queued, waited to be contacted, exchanged messages over days, and eventually reviewed and filed. It was modeled on how a CPA firm operates. The problem is that people go to a CPA's office and sit there until their return is done. They don't drop off their W-2 and come back in 18 days.

Every promise in the marketing was running into friction in the product:

The promise The reality
"Your dedicated expert" Expert availability unknown; callbacks frequently missed
"Work around your schedule" Customers adjusting to expert shift windows
"Check on your progress anytime" No visibility; customers called support to find out
"Taxes done right" Nearly a quarter of dissatisfied customers doubted the accuracy of their return
"Complete your taxes online" 18-day average cycle

The customers bearing the heaviest cost were the ones Full Service was built for: people who wanted to hand their taxes to someone competent and be done. They weren't comparing us to a digital competitor. They were comparing us to sitting in their accountant's office, where you walk in and walk out with your taxes filed.

When we tested the idea of a live, same-session model with prospective customers, the reaction wasn't hesitation. It was recognition. Customers who used in-person accountants or tax stores said the concept made immediate sense. The value proposition wasn't a hard sell. The question was whether we could actually deliver it.

How it started

The seed was a small experiment run by tax prep assistants after the Tax Year 2021 filing deadline. During the extension period, they offered customers the option to skip the multi-day queue and connect directly to a tax expert in the same call. It was manual, operational, and limited in scope. But it proved the core assumption: customers could complete a return in one session when the experience was designed for them to stay.

I worked with product and operations to develop what became the Same Day Full Service model. My role across three years was to design the experience that made it real: the customer-facing product, the tax prep assistant call and handoff, and the tools tax experts used during live sessions. The model only worked when all three were in sync.

The system

Three interlocking experiences.

The customer. The tax prep assistant. The expert. None of them worked alone.

Same Day Full Service wasn't a single product. It was three interlocking experiences that all had to work simultaneously: what the customer saw and did, what the tax prep assistant managed on the call, and what the expert needed to complete the return in a single session. I designed all three.

Setting expectations before they matter

The biggest upstream problem was customers arriving unprepared, or arriving without fully understanding what Full Service involved. More than a fifth dropped out immediately after signing up. Another significant group left within 24 hours of their first call. We were routing people into a flow they weren't ready for.

I redesigned the pre-signup experience to set honest, specific expectations before customers committed: what a live session actually means, roughly how long to budget, what documents to have ready, and why being present while the expert prepares matters. Research surfaced exactly what customers needed to feel confident enough to start: who they'd connect with, when, how long, how much, and why.

Customers who understood what they were getting into were the ones who stayed and completed.

The core tension was honesty versus conversion. More information upfront risked scaring people off. But the research was consistent, and I pushed toward specificity over hedging. Concrete time estimates. Clear descriptions of the session format. Direct language about what the customer needed to have ready.

That work extended into an audit of every version of "how Full Service works" that existed across the product and across teams. Different parts of the organization had developed their own interpretations over time, and customers encountered different descriptions of the same service depending on where they first landed. The audit identified the inconsistencies and became the foundation for a standardized treatment applied across the experience. Misaligned expectations were a measurable driver of early dropout, and fixing them required agreement across teams on what the product actually promised.

Document readiness

Same-day completion was physically impossible without document readiness. An expert can't finish a return in a single session while waiting on documents mid-call.

The document checklist was one of the highest-leverage decisions I made on this project. I surfaced it before the expert connection, framed not as a barrier but as preparation, oriented around the customer's goal of finishing today.

+8 pts conversion lift per 10-point increase in pre-connection doc completion
13 min saved per session when the handoff arrived doc-ready
80% of all documents acquired before expert connection by TY22

The checklist design evolved considerably across three years. By Tax Year 2023 I was driving a full simplification of how document statuses were tracked: collapsing overlapping states, automating where the system could make determinations on its own, eliminating manual steps wherever possible. Each simplification was tied to a measurable reduction in session time. The expert's document management experience and the customer's same-day completion rate were directly connected.

The handoff

In the original model, the tax prep assistant's job ended with intake. I redesigned it to end with handoff.

I rebuilt that experience around a single goal: get the customer document-ready, confirm they were willing to connect now, and transfer them directly to an expert on the same call. The welcome call went from an intake endpoint to a launch point.

Central to this was a readiness confirmation step where customers explicitly affirmed they were ready to connect with a live expert. It introduced a beat into the flow that might look like friction on paper, but it served a real purpose: it separated customers who were genuinely ready from those who weren't, before an expert's time was committed. What happened when we tested removing it is a story worth telling.

I also designed the connect-now / connect-later offer structure that tax prep assistants presented. Not every customer could connect immediately. The design honored both paths: connect now for customers with time right then, schedule for later for those who needed to plan ahead. By Tax Year 2023, the split was 79% connecting immediately, 14% scheduling for later, and 7% booking a specific appointment. The appointment path mattered to a specific customer: someone who wanted the scheduled, one-on-one experience that replicated going into an accountant's office.

Working with operations and service delivery surfaced a recurring boundary question: what belongs in the product, and what belongs in service operations. There was a pull to hardcode assistant guidance into the product interface, which would have made it visible and consistent. But guidance that lives in the product requires an engineering deploy to update. When service delivery needs to adjust what assistants say mid-season, that timeline doesn't work. I pushed for a model where the product provided structure and the service team owned the content within it. That boundary held when operational needs changed mid-season.

Expert-facing tools

A significant share of the leverage lived on the expert side of the product.

I maintained the end-to-end experience documentation for both tax prep assistants and experts, the canonical artifact showing what the product looked like from their side, used to train thousands of tax experts going into each season. Keeping it accurate and current as the product evolved was its own sustained design responsibility.

I directed the redesign of the expert accuracy review flow, the steps experts went through to verify completeness and catch errors before filing. By Tax Year 2023 it had accumulated multiple overlapping check categories that had grown unclear even to the experts using them. I consolidated those into a simpler flow: fewer steps, clearer purpose at each one, automation where the system could make determinations that experts were manually confirming. That redesign ran into real engineering constraints during development, with portions scoped back because the implementation was more complex than initially estimated. I redesigned within those constraints without losing the structural improvement.

Early in this period I caught that an emerging AI workstream had been designed for the customer-facing experience but had no design work started for the tax prep assistant and expert side. I escalated it, got the workstream expanded, and helped stand up the expert-side design effort before the season started. Two-sided services require two-sided design.

The proof

What the failures taught us.

Some of the most important validations came from what we removed.

Removing onboarding

At one point during Tax Year 2023 planning, there was organizational pressure to accelerate the path to expert connection by reducing or eliminating the onboarding experience. The logic was straightforward: fewer screens before connection should mean more completions.

We tested it. Customers who skipped onboarding arrived at experts without documents, without a clear understanding of what Full Service involved, and without a genuine intent to complete. Experts spent session time on intake work the earlier flow was designed to handle. Same-day conversion dropped. The experience degraded for both sides simultaneously.

Speed to connection isn't the same thing as speed to completion.

A customer who connects in two minutes without their documents costs more time than a customer who takes ten minutes to prepare and finishes in one session. The rollback made something explicit that the data had implied: what looked like friction in the onboarding was doing real preparation work, and the rest of the system depended on it.

A software bug reinforced this from the other direction. A defect in Tax Year 2023 caused a portion of customers to skip the readiness confirmation step entirely. The rate of customers who went on to connect with an expert dropped significantly for that group. But among customers who did connect, completion rates were essentially flat. The step wasn't slowing down committed customers. It was identifying uncommitted ones before an expert's time was spent on them.

Designing for two sides at once

The most sustained challenge across all three years wasn't any single feature. It was that I was designing for two completely different users whose needs were frequently in tension.

What felt smooth and momentum-driven to a customer could interrupt an expert mid-preparation. What made an expert faster sometimes added steps to the customer experience. Every design decision had to hold up on both axes, and there was no clean formula for when it didn't.

The broader structural issue was that no single team had a clear mandate over the expert experience end-to-end. I became connective tissue across teams that each owned a piece of it: the customer product, the expert platform, the training organization, the quality team. A significant amount of my design work was making sure the right conversations happened before decisions got made in isolation.

What I learned

  • 01
    Friction that looks removable is often load-bearing. The onboarding, the readiness confirmation step, the document checklist all looked like steps that slowed customers down. All three were doing preparation or filtering work the rest of the system depended on. I learned to ask what a piece of friction is doing before asking how to remove it.
  • 02
    You can't design a service experience from only one side. Some of the highest-leverage work I did was on the expert side: the training documentation, the accuracy review redesign, the status simplification. Expert tool friction was a direct constraint on same-day completion rates. Designing for the customer without designing for the expert would have left half the problem unsolved, and the half that customers feel most.
  • 03
    Institutional memory is a design asset. By Tax Year 2023, I carried detailed context on why nearly every significant decision in the product existed: what we'd tried before, what broke, what the data said, what we'd consciously decided not to do. That context repeatedly protected the team from re-learning expensive lessons. I came to treat documented rationale as something worth maintaining, not just a deliverable artifact.
Ask whether the wait has to exist at all.
Three years of iteration in one direction: make the product worthy of the promise. That meant designing for the customer and the expert simultaneously, treating every piece of friction as a hypothesis rather than a given, and carrying enough institutional memory to protect the team from re-learning what already cost something to learn.
