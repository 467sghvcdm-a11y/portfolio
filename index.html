Approach

Framing

The real problem is rarely the stated one.

I work upstream of the brief. In Business Tax, the program was optimizing starts. I named the broken architecture causing them. A problem with no owner, no PRD, no prior framing. That reframe changed what got built, not just how it looked.
Systems

Build what the next ten screens inherit from.

Pattern libraries, diagnostics, test cascades designed to compound. I don't design a screen, I build the structure that makes the next ten faster and more coherent. Vision and execution in parallel, not in sequence.
Fluency

Know what the data is actually saying.

I track business outcomes, not just UX metrics. I read experiment results and know when a finding is real, when it transfers to another surface, and when a number is being asked to mean something it doesn't.

About Matt Kirkpatrick — public

The shareable layer. Helps a collaborator decide whether to consult me and how to approach me. For the trusted-tier or full file, request via the about-me skill.

How I think

I interrogate what data measures before accepting what it means. When someone presents a result, my first move is to check the metric, the attribution, the population, the filters. The number itself is rarely the issue; the gap between what the number measures and what's being claimed about it is where most decisions go wrong.

I think structurally before I think tactically. A problem in front of me is usually the surface of something deeper. I map the underlying conditions, dependencies, and incentives before iterating on the visible layer.

I build for durability, not just delivery. I look for the pattern that will hold after the immediate project is done. The framework, the principle, the rule that lets future decisions be made faster and more consistently.

I treat research as the primary input to strategy, not a validation step after design decisions are made. Research data feeds directly into the framing that drives design direction. When I co-create with researchers, they're co-authors of the strategy.

I know when influence is capped. There's a point in every program where additional effort doesn't produce additional return, usually because the partnership structure or org context won't carry what I'm trying to build. Recognizing that point and reallocating is part of how I operate, not a failure mode.

I move between registers fluently. Craft, strategy, business, data. Most people operate primarily in one. I read across all of them and know which one the room needs in the moment.

I think end-to-end. Boundaries between surfaces, teams, and stages are where things break. The handoffs, the transitions, the seams.

I separate what I know from what I'm told. Verbal claims, repeated assertions, and inherited assumptions get tested against documented sources.

How I produce work

Show, not tell. I build the artifact that makes the case rather than rely on verbal advocacy alone.

Rigor is load-bearing. My standards are high because the audiences I can't be in front of require it. When my manager has to carry my case into rooms I'm not in, the artifact has to survive that handoff.

Attribution discipline. I separate direct ownership from framework contribution from context. I credit collaborators by name. I won't claim something I didn't drive.

Self-assessment is part of the work. Calibration docs, retros, and growth narratives aren't routine outputs — they're how I make my work legible to people who don't see it daily.

I draft for review, not approval. When I send something, I want a read on whether it lands.

Put me on the hardest problem. The most strategically demanding work is where I'm most useful, even when it's not the most visually complex.

AI is part of how I work every day. I use Claude to pressure-test ideas, accelerate synthesis, and sharpen my thinking before it goes into a room.

What I'm uniquely good at

Holding craft and strategy as the same practice. The craft rigor is what earns the standing to shape strategy.

Naming structural problems with no obvious owner. The ones the system has no natural mechanism to surface.

Building leverage artifacts. Things that change what other people are able to do, not just deliverables.

Data literacy for a designer. Indices, p-values in context, attribution models. I know what a test proves versus what someone is claiming it proves.

Translating design decisions into business language without losing the design logic.

Working cross-functionally in registers other designers don't usually reach.

Working preferences

  • Lead with action, not planning narration
  • Direct is better than diplomatic. Specific is better than general.
  • Close the loop on dropped findings; flag things that go quiet
  • No trailing summaries unless asked
  • No em dashes; use commas, colons, or restructure
  • When something can be tested, test it before reporting it done
