TurboTax Business Tax

The Product Was Broken Before They Got There

RoleDesign Lead Year2025

The problem no one had named

TurboTax had a starts problem in Business Tax. Customers were arriving at the business tax product and not starting. The program realigned around the metric. Fix starts.

But starts wasn't the real problem.

A sole proprietor and a self-employed filer file the exact same tax forms. They have the same tax situation. The only meaningful difference between them is which front door they walked through to reach TurboTax. Sole prop customers were being routed into TTO Premium, a product designed for individual filers, and then asked to navigate a business tax experience that assumed knowledge they didn't have, forced decisions that shouldn't exist, and ended with a pricing surprise they weren't prepared for.

The starts problem was a symptom. Naming that changed everything about how I responded to it.

You can't A/B test your way out of a broken product architecture.

But you can buy time with metric wins while building the case for the real fix.

I ran both tracks simultaneously this season.

The equation

When the program focused on the metric, my first design decision was to build a shared understanding of what was actually happening. Not a task list. Not a roadmap. A diagnosis.

The result was a single line I sketched across the top of the canonical design file that became the operating canvas for the rest of the season:

Auth + Realm creation + Entity selection = Start

Three friction points. Three places where customers were falling out before they ever entered the product. Every test I ran was a response to one of these three variables.

This wasn't a complicated insight. But naming it mattered. It gave the program a shared mental model for what "fixing starts" actually meant at the design level. It turned a panic response into a coordinated one.

What shipped

I tracked every test the program ran in a canonical file: wins, kills, and everything in between. Both design and program management used it to understand what was happening in real time. Four tests won and shipped.

Dual Intent Landing Page

A meaningful share of customers arriving at the business tax landing page had mixed intent: they weren't sure whether they needed business tax or personal tax. I redesigned the combined landing page to clarify the path earlier, before auth, reducing the number of customers who arrived uncertain and abandoned. Launched and rolled to baseline 3/10.

Business Tax Homepage v1 and v2

Two successive iterations on the business tax home page entry experience. Recipe B rolled. Recipe C rolled. Each one a sharper, more confident version of the product's first impression, reducing the cognitive cost of deciding to start.

None of these were the breakout win. That was Dual Auth.

The breakout: Dual Auth

The Dual Auth test performed at twice the baseline rate. Throughout this case study, test results show percentage lift above or below the control group.

The design insight was specific. The realm selection screen (the moment where TurboTax asks customers to choose between personal and business tax) was confusing customers who had QuickBooks-linked businesses. They had a business. TurboTax knew they had a business. But the screen didn't show them that. It presented an abstract fork in the road and asked them to make a choice without giving them the information they needed to make it confidently.

I redesigned the realm chooser to surface their actual business names directly. Not abstract options. Real names they recognized. The choice became confirmation, not decision-making.

The hypothesis was also doing something more important than conversion optimization. By explicitly telling customers they needed to complete their business taxes before their personal taxes, I named the joint-filing dependency for the first time in the flow, reducing the confusion that caused customers to rush through or abandon later in the season.

Design judgment: Reductive GTKMB R1 → R2

Not every test I ran won. The more interesting story is what happened when one lost.

The Reductive Get to Know My Business (GTKMB) test was my attempt to simplify entity selection: the screen where customers had to identify their business structure before TurboTax would let them proceed. Entity selection is one of the three variables in the equation. It was responsible for a meaningful share of abandonment.

R1: the honest loss

The first version presented three tiles: Single-member LLC, Multi-member LLC, S-corp.

46% of customers selected a tile and continued. 47% abandoned.

The catchall screen caught another 42% into business tax, but 45% abandoned there too. The test failed. But the failure contained the real insight.

When the data came back, it showed the actual distribution of entity types:

58% Sole prop
11% Partnership
10% "I'm not sure"  ·  90% of those didn't start
9% S-corp
0.02% C-corp

The vast majority of customers were sole proprietors. The screen was asking them to make a decision they didn't need to make. The entity question had been built for the product's internal taxonomy, not for the customer's self-understanding.

R2: the reframe

"If we provide an 'I'm not sure' option, and then route all customers who select that into the sole prop SKU, reduce and simplify the content, reduce the choices, and lower cognitive burden, then we will reduce abandonment and increase starts."

The "I'm not sure" routing is the key design decision. Most onboarding flows force a choice and treat uncertainty as a failure state. I treated uncertainty as information. 58% of customers were sole props. 10% didn't know what they were. Routing uncertain customers to the most likely correct outcome wasn't a hack. It was designing for the actual distribution instead of the assumed one.

Chapter 2  ·  The bigger picture
Running two tracks simultaneously.
While the tests were moving metrics, a patterns library, a diagnostic, and a product vision were being built in parallel.

The work underneath the cascade

The four shipped tests were the visible layer. Running in parallel, all season, was a complete in-product design system.

I started the Patterns: Assisted Business Tax file on January 9th with cards and levels scaffolding. By February 11th it had become "Business: modernized landing tables with next best action." The addition of "next best action" signals the shift from a product that collects data to a product that guides behavior. By April 23rd the file contained fully designed Prove identity verification flows: the automated business info collection concept from the vision work, moved into production-quality execution.

The vision and the craft work were running in parallel. The big swings weren't sitting in a strategy deck while the patterns file did something else. I was building both simultaneously.

Five screen template categories shipped as ready for dev by end of season: Section onramp, Section topic launcher (3 variants), Worksheet (3 variants), Overview Collection (4 variants), Data collection: Question Answer (3 variants).

What the tests were responding to

Every test I ran was a response to a documented problem. I built the FY26 sole prop onboarding target state file to map the complete customer journey (from off-platform discovery through auth through entity selection) with problem annotations at each friction point and a reframed design question at each one.

This wasn't a wish list. It was a diagnostic. The journey map identified the structural problems. The test cascade addressed the ones solvable within the season. The target state documented the ones that required more runway.

  • 01 Front doors: How might we match customers to business industry and state-specific tax experts?
  • 02 Shell / Stationary: How might we take a tax platform approach to onboarding that helps customers wayfare to the right solution, no matter where they started?
  • 03 Auth: How might we avoid price shock at checkout?
  • 04 GTKMB: How might we shift the burden of entity selection off the customer and onto TurboTax?
  • 05 GTKMB: How might we provide guidance on when and how to file business and personal taxes together?

Question #4 was answered directly by Reductive GTKMB: the "I'm not sure → sole prop" routing was a literal execution of "shift the burden of entity selection off the customer." Question #5 was answered by Dual Auth: naming the joint-filing dependency explicitly for the first time in the flow.

Questions #1, #2, and #3 required a different kind of change: rethinking the front door architecture, the onboarding model, and the pricing reveal. I documented them, presented them, and they became the foundation for the vision work.

The vision: what should be built

I started the big swings work from four provocations.

  • 01 Dual login evolves into a post-authentication product experience: a unified hub where business and personal returns are peer entities, not a binary modal fork at the start of every session.
  • 02 Business profile creation is eliminated. Three automated paths (Prove identity verification, website/Etsy URL, prior-year PDF) all converge on a pre-populated "Review and verify" screen. The customer's job becomes confirmation, not data entry.
  • 03 There is no more GTKMB. Entity type is inferred from automated business info collection, not asked.
  • 04 Instead of asking the customer for their entity type, TurboTax tells them what it is. Recommendation-driven pricing before checkout. The pricing moment moves from surprise to recommendation.

These aren't aspirational positions. They're the logical endpoint of the diagnostic. Entity selection causes 47% abandonment because customers don't know their entity type, so eliminate entity selection. Realm creation introduces unnecessary friction before the product knows enough about the customer to route them correctly, so eliminate realm creation. The dual login forks customers at the wrong moment, so eliminate the fork.

I followed every provocation through to production-quality comps. I mapped six distinct customer entry paths (shopping for personal tax, shopping for business, shopping for sole prop, returning customer, optional upgrade, required upgrade) all converging on the same automated onboarding moment. Different front doors. One product.

The post-onboarding vision

I started from three customer problems, stated plainly. Sole proprietor customers are treated the same as TTO Premium customers, even though they have fundamentally different needs. Customers are afraid because they think taxes with business income are complicated, and as income grows, that fear compounds. And there is willingness to pay for a solution that builds confidence in tax outcomes, but the product wasn't designed to earn it.

My design response was a complete end-to-end product redesign. Not wireframes or concepts. Production-quality screens for every stage of the experience. Business info, income, and expenses flows. Personal info, income, and deductions, all the way through to a deductions outcome screen designed as a resolution, not a handoff. The experience ends at the emotional outcome, not the last data entry field.

I designed the personal tax flows inside the business tax file. That wasn't scope expansion. It was the argument made concrete. A sole prop files one return. The product should feel like one return.

I grounded every design decision in research. Customer findings were cited at the relevant moments in the flow, not as decoration, but as the evidence chain connecting customer problem to design response. Each question was traceable back to a finding.

Name the structural problem earlier and louder.
